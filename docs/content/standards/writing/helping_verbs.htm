Helping Verbs

Summary:

Helping verbs, also known as auxiliary or "Be" verbs are non-active and are primarily used to help out the main verb in a sentence. They appear before the main verb and bring detail to how time is portrayed in a sentence. They can also be used to show expectation, probability, obligation, potential, and directions.

While it sounds like helping verbs really are helpful, they do have a drawback. The additional detail helping verbs bring can make sentences more difficult to translate, or for some non-english speaking users, to fully understand. TComm strives to avoid using helping verbs, and instead uses a single active verb whenever possible.

There are 23 helping or auxiliary verbs in common use. These verbs fall into two categories: primary helping verbs and modal helping verbs.

Primary

Primary helping verbs can be used as a main verb or a helping verb. There are three types of primary helping verbs: the "Be" verbs, the verb Have and its variations, and the verb Do and its variations.

"Be" Verbs are inactive verbs that show a state of being or existing.

  • is - Holley is an excellent writer.
  • are - They are eating pizza and salad and drinking lemon-lime Kool-Aid.
  • am - I am going to Switzerland.
  • was - Bob was hungry.
  • were - We were going to order a pizza, but got two instead.
  • be - I'll be in Zurich soon.
  • being - Cynthia was being her regular, charming self.
  • been - I've been to Europe.

•Have is used to make perfect tenses, which shows action that is already completed.

  • have - I have two slice of pizza.
  • had - Bob had more slices than Suzie.
  • has - Sally has gone to get the pizza.

•Do is variable and can be used several ways including making a negative, asking a question, showing emphasis, and replacing a verb.

  • do - I do not like Brussels sprouts.
  • do - Do you like Brussels sprouts?
  • do - I do like peas and carrots.
  • did - Did you hear about Sally, Bob, and the pizza?
  • does - I like peas and carrots more than Bob does.
  • does - Sally and Bob like pizza more than Cynthia does.

Modal

Modal helping verbs help modify the meaning of the main verb by expressing necessity or possibility.

  • can - Susie can order a pizza.
  • could - We could fly to Rome tomorrow.
  • will - Will you have wine with dinner?
  • would - I would like a glass of beer.
  • shall - Shall I order a pizza now?
  • should - You should order two pizzas.
  • may - Susie and Sally may veto the pizza.
  • might - Bob might go ahead and order it anyway.
  • must - We must have pizza for dinner.
  • ought to - Sally ought to learn how to surf.

Semi-Modal

In addition to Modal helping verbs, there are also three "Semi-Modal" helping verbs, which can be used as a modal helping verb, or a main verb.

  • need - Bob needs to pay for the pizza.
  • dare - I dare Susie to run a zip line.
  • used to - Holley used to run, but now she jogs.
